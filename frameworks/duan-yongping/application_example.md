# How To Use The Duan Yongping Lens

Date: 2026-05-05

This is a practical guide for using the Duan Yongping-style lens in company
research. It is not an attempt to impersonate Duan Yongping. It is a structured
checklist based on the ideas currently captured in `investor_agents.md`.

## Core Job

The Duan lens should answer:

```text
Do we really understand this business, why users choose it, whether the culture
helps it keep doing the right thing, and what we should refuse to do?
```

It should run before valuation screens. This prevents the research process from
starting with "cheap" and then rationalizing a business we do not understand.

## Step-By-Step Use

### 1. One-Sentence Business Understanding

Question:

- Can I explain how the company creates value and earns money in one plain
  sentence?

Pass:

- The business model is understandable without jargon.
- Revenue, user value, and competitive advantage connect naturally.

Fail:

- The thesis depends mainly on buzzwords such as AI, TAM, platform, ecosystem,
  or optionality.

### 2. User Value

Question:

- Why do users/customers choose this product instead of alternatives?

Evidence to collect:

- Retention, repeat purchase, usage frequency.
- Customer reviews or developer/community feedback.
- Price increases without losing users.
- Product quality versus competitors.

Fail:

- The company grows only because of subsidies, financial engineering, channel
  pressure, or one-time hype.

### 3. Culture And Incentives

Question:

- Does the company keep doing the right thing when short-term incentives push it
  the other way?

Evidence to collect:

- Founder/operator behavior.
- Capital allocation history.
- Whether management admits mistakes.
- Treatment of users, employees, suppliers, and shareholders.

Fail:

- Management optimizes narrative over product.
- Culture depends on slogans but incentives reward the opposite.

### 4. Differentiation

Question:

- What is hard for competitors to copy?

Possible answers:

- Brand trust.
- Product experience.
- Distribution.
- Cost structure.
- Data or network effects.
- Culture and execution cadence.

Fail:

- The company only has a temporary feature lead.
- Competitors can buy the same advantage with enough capital.

### 5. Long-Term Runway

Question:

- If this is a good business, can it still compound for many years?

Evidence to collect:

- Market size that is not purely fantasy TAM.
- New product adjacency with proof.
- International expansion with local evidence.
- Reinvestment opportunities at good returns.

Fail:

- Growth depends on a single product already near saturation.
- Expansion requires a worse business model than the core.

### 6. Do-Not-Do List

Question:

- What should we refuse to do here?

Common answers:

- Do not buy because everyone says it is cheap.
- Do not buy what we cannot explain.
- Do not average down if the business quality breaks.
- Do not let short-term price action replace business evidence.
- Do not over-size before information completeness is high.

## Example: AI Hardware Supplier

Facts:

- Revenue grew 3x in two years.
- Two largest customers are 55% of revenue.
- Gross margin rose, but R&D and capacity spending remain high.
- Stock is up 6x.
- Market expects three more years of very high growth.
- Customers may self-design part of the component stack.

Duan lens output:

### What The Lens Likes

- There may be real user/customer value if customers keep buying because the
  product improves their own economics.
- If the product is mission-critical and hard to replace, the company may have
  more than a short cycle advantage.
- High R&D can be good if it protects product leadership rather than hiding weak
  economics.

### What The Lens Worries About

- Customer concentration means we may not fully understand who has bargaining
  power.
- A 6x stock move can tempt us to confuse price momentum with business quality.
- If customers can self-design substitutes, the advantage may be less durable
  than current margins suggest.
- "AI demand is huge" is not enough. We need to know why this company specifically
  keeps winning.

### Questions Before Any Buy Decision

- What problem does the product solve better than alternatives?
- Are customers buying because the product is best, or because supply is tight?
- Can customers switch or self-design within 2-3 years?
- Does management allocate capital for long-term product advantage or short-term
  revenue capture?
- Are gross margins sustainable once supply normalizes?
- What evidence would show culture/product quality is deteriorating?

### Provisional Verdict

Watchlist, not automatic buy.

Reason:

- The business may be excellent, but the lens cannot approve it until we
  understand customer value, switching risk, and durability. The high stock price
  increases the need for patience and evidence.

### Kill Criteria

- Key customers reduce purchases because in-house designs are good enough.
- Gross margin improvement reverses while capacity spending remains high.
- Management starts using vague AI language instead of concrete product and
  customer economics.
- Growth remains high but free cash flow quality deteriorates.

## How This Differs From Buffett/Graham

Buffett asks:

- Is this a durable business with owner earnings and a moat?

Graham asks:

- What is the conservative value and downside protection?

Duan asks first:

- Do I really understand the business and users?
- Is the company doing the right thing?
- What should I refuse to do?

So in this project, Duan goes first to prevent false understanding. Buffett and
Graham come later as durability and valuation guardrails.
