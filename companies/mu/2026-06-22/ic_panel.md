## Warren Buffett — lens — "wonderful business at a fair price; circle of competence; cyclical caution"

**Lens:** Buffett buys wonderful businesses at fair prices and is deeply wary of commodity/cyclical businesses
(his steel/airline scars). On MU:
- The business has become genuinely wonderful *at this moment* (fortress balance sheet, huge FCF, real moat).
  But memory is, at its core, a **commodity-cyclical** — the kind of business where "what the wise man does in
  the beginning, the fool does in the end" (chasing peak earnings).
- A net-cash balance sheet earns Buffett's respect (survivability). But buying at a peak multiple on peak
  earnings is the opposite of a margin of safety.
- **Conclusion (lens):** a wonderful-right-now but cyclical business at a price that prices the peak as
  permanent → outside the "fair price" requirement. **No new money; would not short a good business either.**

## Charlie Munger — lens — "invert; avoid the obvious way to lose; psychology of the crowd"

**Lens:** Invert — how do I lose money here? By buying a cyclical at the top because the recent numbers are
dazzling and everyone is euphoric (the "social proof + recency" trap Munger warned about). The HBM-sold-out,
+850%-in-a-year, "Strong Buy" narrative is exactly the crowd psychology that precedes cyclical tops.
- The avoid-the-obvious-mistake move is to NOT pay an all-time-high price for a business whose 74% margins the
  whole industry is racing to compete away.
- **Conclusion (lens):** "It's a fine business. The mistake would be confusing a fine business with a fine
  *investment at this price*. I'd sit on my hands." **WATCH.**

## Howard Marks — lens — "where are we in the cycle? price vs value; second-level thinking"

**Lens:** Marks's central question is literally "where are we in the cycle?" — and a memory stock at an
all-time high on record margins after a +850% run is, by his framework, **late-cycle by every observable
marker** (euphoric sentiment, "this time HBM is different" narrative, price above even bullish targets).
- Second-level thinking: the first-level view is "AI memory supercycle, sold out, buy." The second-level view
  is "everyone knows that, it's in the price at 2.7x normalized fair value; the risk/reward is now asymmetric
  to the downside."
- Marks doesn't call tops, but he sizes for where we are: at this point in the cycle, you reduce risk, you
  don't add it.
- **Conclusion (lens):** **WATCH new money; trim/reduce risk in an existing position** — "move toward the exit
  as others move toward euphoria."

## Seth Klarman — lens — "margin of safety is everything; absolute, not relative, value"

**Lens:** Klarman's non-negotiable is margin of safety. At $1,184.88 vs ~$165 bear / ~$440 base / ~$950 bull
normalized fair value, there is **negative** margin of safety on any reasonable normalized basis (the price
exceeds even the bull case). Relative cheapness ("only 11x forward!") is irrelevant — that's a relative-value
argument on peak earnings, exactly what Klarman rejects.
- A cyclical with no margin of safety at the top is the antithesis of a Klarman position.
- **Conclusion (lens):** **REJECT for new money** (no margin of safety, full stop); the most conservative of
  the five lenses.
