## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #1: Mako Installed-Base Pulls Implant Sales (THE CORE CAREER-RELEVANT MECHANISM)

### The Structure

Stryker's Mako SmartRobotics platform has 3,000+ cumulative installed systems globally (milestone reached Q4 2025) with 1M+ robotic knee procedures and 2M+ total robotic procedures performed to date [facts.md E30, E32]. The analyst thesis — plausible, consistent with the general medtech razor/razorblade model, but **not company-confirmed** — is that a Mako placement effectively locks a hospital into Stryker-brand knee/hip implants (e.g., the Triathlon knee system) for procedures performed on the robot, because: (a) the robotic software/planning workflow is calibrated to Stryker implant geometries, (b) surgeons trained on the Mako-Triathlon workflow face real switching friction to use a competitor's implant on the same robot (if even possible), and (c) hospital purchasing/supply-chain relationships tend to consolidate around the primary robotic-platform vendor.

**Durability**: If confirmed, this would be a GENUINELY DEEPER lock-in than GEHC's installed-base service annuity, because it ties a recurring, high-margin CONSUMABLE (the implant) directly to the CAPITAL EQUIPMENT (the robot) at the point of use, rather than merely creating switching friction around a service contract. Unlike GEHC's service annuity (where independent service organizations are a known competitive channel), there is no equivalent "independent Mako implant supplier" concept in sources reviewed this run — the linkage, if real, would be closer to a closed ecosystem.

**Evidence gap (the central unresolved question)**: No source reviewed this run — including the 10-K — discloses an implant attach-rate, an ASP premium for on-robot procedures, or a Mako-specific revenue segment. This is THE single largest quantification gap in this dossier, directly analogous to GEHC's unquantified AI-monetization gap (facts.md O7 in the GEHC dossier). **Top priority for next refresh**: any company disclosure, analyst channel-check, or hospital-purchasing-data source that could confirm or refute the pull-through mechanism with an actual number.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #2: Surgeon Training + OR Workflow Redesign — THE SWITCHING-COST MOAT

### The Structure

Every Mako placement requires: hospital capital commitment, surgeon retraining on the robotic workflow, and OR process redesign to accommodate the pre-operative planning and intra-operative robotic-assistance steps. This is a genuine, multi-month-to-multi-year switching-cost investment on both Stryker's side (training infrastructure, field support) and the hospital's side (staff time, workflow disruption during transition) — a real barrier against a hospital simply switching to Zimmer's ROSA (~1,500 installs, roughly half of Mako's base, facts.md E38) or a new entrant once the initial commitment is made.

**Why this matters for the China thesis**: This is the mechanism most directly analogous to (and in direct competition with) China's domestic robotics entrants. Tinavi's Tirobot (NMPA-cleared for knee application in 2023), MicroPort MedBot's OrthoBot/Honghu platforms, and other domestic entrants (Weigao, AK Medical, Chunli, facts.md E40) are, in principle, subject to the SAME surgeon-training/workflow-lock-in dynamic — meaning whichever platform (Stryker's Mako or a domestic Chinese alternative) achieves early scale in a given hospital system may enjoy a durable, self-reinforcing advantage in that specific market. **The critical unresolved question**: has Stryker achieved meaningful Mako installed-base scale in China specifically, or have domestic entrants already captured first-mover advantage in Chinese hospitals via VBP-tender alignment (the same playbook that worked for United Imaging against GEHC)? No source reviewed this run answers this directly — flagged as the top China-specific research priority (facts.md O3).

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #3 (INVERTED — this is a bottleneck AGAINST SYK): China VBP Tendering + Domestic Robotics NMPA Clearance

### The Structure

China's volume-based procurement (VBP) system has already imposed a quantified, if modest, growth-rate headwind on Stryker: −1.4% in 2024, −1.5% guided for 2025 [facts.md E42]. This is the SAME mechanism (centralized government tendering, potential local-manufacture procurement preference) documented in the GEHC/United Imaging dossier, applied here to orthopedic implants and — potentially, though unconfirmed — to robotics specifically.

### The Evidence (thinner than the GEHC/United Imaging comparison — this is the key gap)

- Stryker discloses a **growth-RATE** VBP impact (−1.4%/−1.5%) but **no revenue-dollar or country-% figure for China** [facts.md E42, O3] — materially less resolved than GEHC's clean $2.03B/9.85%-of-revenue China fact.
- China domestic orthopedic-robotics competitors are named and real (Tinavi/Tirobot NMPA-cleared 2023 for knee, MicroPort OrthoBot/Honghu, plus adjacent implant makers Weigao/AK Medical/Chunli, facts.md E40) — but **no source this run quantifies their installed base, revenue, or share trajectory** the way United Imaging's 31% CAGR and 18.2% share were quantified for GEHC. This asymmetry means the SYK China-robotics thesis is currently LESS decision-useful than the GEHC China-imaging thesis, purely due to an evidence gap, not because the underlying risk is necessarily smaller.
- Directionally, the mechanism is identical to the GEHC case: government-tender-driven pricing pressure + domestic manufacturers building NMPA-cleared, price-competitive alternatives. Whether this has already meaningfully eroded Stryker's China robotics/implant position (as it demonstrably has for GEHC's China imaging position) is the single most important open question for the career-relevant thesis and cannot be answered with the evidence gathered this run.

### Why This is THE Career-Relevant Finding (Alongside Bottleneck #1)

This dossier presents a genuinely different evidentiary situation than GEHC: where GEHC's China erosion was CONFIRMED and QUANTIFIED (−15% YoY, a clean financial fingerprint), Stryker's China exposure is DIRECTIONALLY SUGGESTED (VBP growth-rate drag is real and disclosed) but NOT QUANTIFIED at the dollar or robotics-specific level. **For Nick's China med-device + AI/robotics competitive research, the correct interpretive stance is: do not assume Stryker's China robotics position is either safe or already eroding — flag this explicitly as the single highest-value research gap to close on the next refresh**, given that the analogous GEHC/United Imaging case shows this exact playbook (VBP + domestic NMPA-cleared alternative + local-for-local preference) has already worked once against a comparable global medtech incumbent.

## Bottleneck Durability vs Trend — The Core Tension

**The bull case**: Stryker's Mako franchise has a genuinely larger, better-quantified installed base (3,000+ systems, 1M+/2M+ procedures) than any single domestic Chinese competitor disclosed in sources reviewed this run, plus a clear multi-generation platform roadmap (Hip Revision already shipping, Spine and Shoulder in the pipeline) that is actively expanding the addressable procedure base faster than a domestic entrant starting from a single knee-application clearance (Tinavi, 2023) could plausibly match in the near term. FY2026 guidance was MAINTAINED (not cut) despite a real operational disruption, signaling management confidence in the underlying demand trajectory.

**The bear case / kill path**: If China's domestic robotics entrants (Tinavi, MicroPort, or others) replicate the United Imaging playbook — VBP-tender alignment, local-manufacture preference, rapid NMPA-cleared product iteration — Stryker's China robotics/implant position could erode in the same way GEHC's China imaging position has, but this dossier cannot currently confirm or deny whether that erosion has already begun, because Stryker's disclosure (a growth-rate headwind, not a revenue fact) is thinner than GEHC's. Separately, if Stryker's own contemplated move into soft-tissue robotics (E39) dilutes management focus or capital away from defending/expanding the core orthopedic-robotics moat, that would be a genuinely different (self-inflicted, not competitive) risk to the current franchise.

**The key open question**: Is Stryker's China VBP/robotics-competitive exposure closer to GEHC's already-realized −15% erosion, or is it a smaller, well-contained drag (−1.4% to −1.5% at the growth-RATE level, company-wide, not robotics-specific)? **This cannot be answered with the evidence gathered this run** and should be the explicit, named top research priority for the next SYK dossier refresh — directly comparable in spirit to the GEHC dossier's AI-monetization gap as "the single most important unresolved question."
