## What SYK Sells: Two Segments (as-filed FY2025 structure)

### 1. MedSurg and Neurotechnology (larger segment, 62% of FY2025 revenue at $15,647M, +15.7% reported / +10.7% organic)

**Instruments** (surgical power tools, waste management, smoke evacuation), **Endoscopy** (video imaging, communications, sports medicine), **Medical** (emergency care/EMS cots & stretchers, Physio-Control/LIFEPAK defibrillators, patient handling, Sage disposables), **Neurovascular** (stroke — coils, stent retrievers for ischemic/hemorrhagic stroke), and **Neuro Cranial** (neurosurgical, ENT). This is the faster-growing, more acquisition-fed half of the business — reported growth (+15.7%) running well ahead of organic growth (+10.7%), consistent with Stryker's active bolt-on M&A cadence (~$5.0B/year, facts.md E15) layering acquired revenue on top of organic growth.

**Mechanism**: Broad portfolio of surgical instruments, imaging, emergency/critical-care equipment, and consumables (Sage disposables, defibrillator batteries/pads) sold to hospitals and EMS systems. Mix of capital equipment (defibrillators, imaging), disposables/consumables (Sage, coils, stent retrievers), and service — a genuinely diversified revenue base less dependent on any single procedure volume than the Orthopaedics segment.

### 2. Orthopaedics (38% of FY2025 revenue at $9,469M, +4.3% reported / +9.5% organic — see divergence flag below)

**Knees, Hips, Trauma & Extremities, Other Ortho, Spine** (spinal implants + enabling technology), and — critically — **Mako SmartRobotics** (robotic-arm-assisted surgery platform sitting within this segment). This is the segment where Stryker's most distinctive competitive asset — Mako — lives, and the segment most directly exposed to China VBP pricing pressure (facts.md E42-E43) and domestic-robotics competition (E40).

**NOTE on segment growth divergence** (facts.md I4/O2): Orthopaedics' reported growth (+4.3%) trails its organic growth (+9.5%) by ~5.2 points — the opposite pattern from MedSurg & Neurotechnology, where reported growth exceeds organic. This is unusual and unresolved in sources reviewed this run; possible explanations include an unusual FX mix, a portfolio divestiture, or a data-extraction issue in the secondary source used. Flagged as an explicit OPEN item, not glossed over.

## The Core Mechanism: Installed Robotic Base + Implant Pull-Through + Instrument Recurring Revenue

Stryker's Orthopaedics business follows the classic med-tech "razor/razorblade" pattern, but with Mako adding a THIRD layer of lock-in beyond the traditional two:

1. **Capital equipment sale (Mako robot placement)** — the "razor," a genuine capital-equipment sale with real installed-base lock-in once a hospital commits to training surgeons and building OR workflow around the platform (facts.md E30-E34)
2. **Implant "razorblade" pull-through** — the analyst thesis (facts.md I5, O4) is that once a hospital has a Mako robot, on-robot knee/hip procedures are performed using Stryker-brand implants (e.g., the Triathlon knee system), creating a recurring, procedure-linked revenue stream tied to the installed base. **This mechanism is NOT quantified in the 10-K or IR materials reviewed this run** — it is a well-reasoned, plausible analyst thesis consistent with the general medtech model, but should be explicitly flagged as unverified company disclosure, not treated as an audited fact.
3. **Instrument/consumable recurring revenue** — surgical instruments, disposables (Sage), and implant hardware sold per-procedure regardless of the specific robotic platform used, providing a baseline recurring revenue layer even independent of Mako-specific attach.

This differs from GEHC's three-layer model (capital equipment / service annuity / OEM-agnostic consumables) in one key respect: Stryker's "consumable" layer (implants) is NOT OEM-agnostic the way GEHC's PDx contrast agents are — Stryker implants are (per the analyst thesis) specifically tied to Stryker's own robotic platform, making the lock-in tighter but also more concentrated and more exposed if a competing robotic platform (Zimmer ROSA, or a future Stryker move into soft-tissue robotics) disrupts the linkage.

## Geographic Mix (thinner disclosure than the GEHC exemplar — flagged OPEN)

Unlike GEHC (which had a clean, quantified geographic-revenue footnote resolving China to ~10% of total revenue), Stryker's China disclosure in sources reviewed this run is limited to a **growth-rate VBP headwind** (−1.4% in 2024, −1.5% guided 2025, facts.md E42) rather than a country-level revenue dollar figure or percentage of total. An Asia-Pacific regional estimate (~$2.02B, ~8.9% of FY2024 revenue, facts.md E41) exists but conflates China with the broader region. **This is the single largest evidence gap versus the GEHC dossier's resolved China fact and the top research priority for the next SYK refresh** (facts.md O3).

## Why This Matters for the AI/Robotics/China Career Lens

Stryker's Mako franchise represents the closest orthopedic-specific analog to the broader "installed-base robotic moat" thesis being tracked across this coverage universe. Unlike GEHC (premium imaging hardware + AI software layer, competing against United Imaging's fast-follower hardware) or ISRG (soft-tissue robotics, no orthopedic overlap today), Stryker's position is: **~85%+ estimated share of an orthopedic-robotics category it effectively created and still dominates** (facts.md E36, analyst estimate), now facing two distinct emerging pressures — (1) **China domestic robotics entrants** (Tinavi, MicroPort OrthoBot/Honghu, Weigao, AK Medical, Chunli — facts.md E40) building NMPA-cleared, price-competitive alternatives in-market, analogous to United Imaging's playbook against GEHC; and (2) **Stryker's own stated evaluation of the soft-tissue robotics adjacency** (~50 startups, facts.md E39), which would be a move INTO Intuitive Surgical's and Medtronic Hugo's territory rather than a defense of Stryker's existing turf. The moat_map.md and bottleneck_map.md develop both tensions in depth.
