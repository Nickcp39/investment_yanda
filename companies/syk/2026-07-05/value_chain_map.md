## Upstream → SYK → Downstream

**Upstream inputs**:
- Precision-machined metal components (titanium, cobalt-chrome implant alloys), robotics/motion-control hardware and sensors (Mako arm), electronics/semiconductors (Mako computing/imaging stack, LIFEPAK defibrillators, endoscopy video systems), specialty polymers (Sage disposables), and contract manufacturing for lower-complexity instruments.
- Tariff exposure across this input base is explicitly guided as a ~$400M FY2026 headwind (facts.md E23), weighted mostly to H1 2026 — a genuine, quantified cost-side pressure analogous to GEHC's tariff drag.
- IT/software supply chain — the March 2026 cyber incident (facts.md E21) demonstrates a real operational dependency on internal IT systems for order-to-shipment execution; a ~3-week disruption cascaded into a ~$375M deferred-sales impact, underscoring that Stryker's manufacturing/distribution pipeline has a genuine single-point-of-failure risk in its own IT infrastructure.

**SYK internal value-add**:
- R&D / device engineering ($1,623M FY2025, facts.md E14) — robotics platform development (Mako 4, Hip Revision, Spine, Shoulder, RPS), implant design, surgical instrument engineering, digital/software layer for the robotic platform.
- Manufacturing (implants, instruments, capital equipment) — high-precision, regulatory-intensive (FDA/CE-mark/NMPA clearance required for each new indication/product).
- Direct + channel sales force with deep surgeon-training relationships — Mako specifically requires surgeon training and OR workflow redesign, a genuine switching-cost-creating investment on both Stryker's and the hospital's side.
- M&A integration engine — ~$5.0B/year acquisition cash spend (facts.md E15), 60+ deals under CEO Lobo (E46) — an internal capability (deal sourcing, integration, cross-sell) that is itself a distinct value-chain competency, not just capital deployment.

**Downstream customers**:
- Hospitals / health systems (Mako robot capital-equipment buyers; implant and instrument buyers on a per-procedure basis) — long-lived capital equipment (Mako) creates multi-year workflow lock-in once surgeons are trained.
- Orthopedic surgeons directly (training, workflow, and — per the analyst pull-through thesis — implant-brand preference once trained on a specific robotic platform).
- EMS / emergency-response systems (Physio-Control/LIFEPAK defibrillators, patient-handling equipment) — a distinct downstream channel from the hospital-OR channel.
- Government/public health systems (China VBP tenders specifically — a distinct, price-sensitive procurement channel exerting a quantified −1.4% to −1.5% growth-rate drag, facts.md E42).

## Bottleneck Candidates (developed further in bottleneck_map.md)

1. **Mako installed base + surgeon training** — every Mako placement requires hospital capital commitment + surgeon retraining + OR workflow redesign. This is a genuine, multi-year switching-cost bottleneck favoring Stryker's existing 3,000+ installed base (facts.md E30) against any new entrant, domestic or international.
2. **Regulatory approval (FDA/NMPA/CE-mark) for each new robotic indication** — Mako Hip Revision (FDA-cleared March 2025, facts.md E34) and the pending Mako Shoulder (2026 launch target) each require independent regulatory clearance. This is a time-based moat against fast-followers on any SINGLE indication, but China's NMPA pathway may favor domestic entrants (Tinavi's Tirobot cleared for knee application in 2023, facts.md E40) on their own timeline — the CHANNEL bottleneck point developed further in bottleneck_map.md.
3. **China VBP tendering + local-manufacture preference** — the inverse-bottleneck pattern also seen in GEHC's China dynamic: centralized government tendering plus (per general policy context, not SYK-quantified this run) an increasing tilt toward domestic-manufacture criteria works AGAINST Stryker's implant/robotics pricing power in China specifically.
4. **Internal IT infrastructure as a single point of failure** — the March 2026 cyber incident (facts.md E21) is direct, realized evidence that Stryker's order-to-shipment pipeline has a genuine operational bottleneck risk sitting inside its OWN infrastructure, not just external competitive or regulatory pressure. This is a distinct risk category from GEHC's supply-chain/tariff-driven cost bottlenecks — it is an execution/resilience bottleneck, and the fact that it already materialized once (with a quantified $375M impact) makes it a realized risk, not a hypothetical one.
