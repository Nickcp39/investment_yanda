| **Company-disclosed leverage ratio** | not disclosed this run | not disclosed this run | **3.8x gross debt / adj EBITDA; 3.5x net debt / adj EBITDA** |

**Read**: Debt rose from FY2025's $39.385B to Q1 2026's $43.2B (+$3.8B), while cash collapsed from $9.852B to $3.3B (−$6.5B) — the swing is driven almost entirely by the **$8.875B cash Clario acquisition** closing 2026-03-24. This is a real, sizeable, single-quarter leverage increase. Unlike GEHC (where net debt had to be manually derived across sources), **TMO directly discloses its own leverage ratio** (3.5x net debt/adjusted EBITDA) — a genuine transparency positive that also makes the leverage level easier to benchmark: 3.5x is a moderate-to-elevated leverage level for an investment-grade-profile company, not alarming on its own, but a level that warrants monitoring for a credible deleveraging trajectory, especially layered on top of the FY2025 FCF decline noted above. The concurrent microbiology divestiture ($1.1B, expected H2 2026 close) will provide some offsetting deleveraging capacity.

**Verdict**: Leverage is **moderate, transparently disclosed, and M&A-explained** (not a distress signal), but the DIRECTION (rising debt, falling cash, declining FCF, all in the same period) is a genuine, monitorable combination — materially better-disclosed than GEHC's equivalent finding, but not categorically less real as a risk to track.

## Financial Quality Verdict

**GOOD TO VERY GOOD — meaningfully stronger and more stable than the GEHC exemplar on margin trend, though with its own distinct FCF and leverage flags.** Revenue has credibly reaccelerated post-COVID-normalization with STABLE-to-slightly-improving adjusted operating margin (22.6%→22.7%), a materially better trajectory than GEHC's three-period compression. However: (1) FY2025 FCF declined −13.5% YoY despite revenue/earnings growth, cause unconfirmed, (2) leverage rose materially in Q1 2026 (though company-transparently-disclosed at 3.5x net debt/EBITDA) driven by the large Clario acquisition, and (3) the Analytical Instruments segment is the one area of outright organic decline. This financial profile supports a "genuinely solid, actively-managed compounder absorbing a large, guidance-embedded acquisition" read — more favorable than GEHC's "moderately-levered incumbent absorbing cost headwinds" finding, but not a flawless "clean compounder" verdict given the FCF and leverage flags requiring further monitoring.
