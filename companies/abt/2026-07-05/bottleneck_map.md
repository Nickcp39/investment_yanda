## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #1: FreeStyle Libre Installed Base + Prescription/Reimbursement Lock-In (STRONGEST BOTTLENECK IN THE PORTFOLIO)

### The Structure
Once a person with diabetes is established on FreeStyle Libre, switching to a competing CGM (Dexcom, Medtronic) requires: (a) a new physician prescription, (b) insurance/payer reauthorization (often a meaningful administrative friction point in US healthcare specifically), (c) behavior change (different app, different sensor insertion mechanics, different alert/alarm calibration the user has already learned). This is a genuine, real-world switching cost — not merely a marketing narrative.

**Evidence**: ~8M-user global installed base, ~52.83% global CGM revenue share [facts.md E50-E51]. Sensor replacement cadence (~14 days) means Abbott captures a recurring, near-subscription-like revenue stream from this installed base continuously, not just at initial sale.

**Durability**: Real but **NOT absolute** — Dexcom is demonstrably NOT losing the US market (48.5% Abbott vs 44.7% Dexcom — closer to a duopoly split than Abbott dominance) [E51], and Dexcom's G7 has a measurable technical accuracy edge (MARD 8.2% vs Libre 3's 9.2%) on at least one independent metric [E53]. This means the switching-cost bottleneck is real enough to sustain Abbott's large installed base, but not strong enough to prevent Dexcom from continuing to win a meaningful share of NEW patient starts, especially where clinicians prioritize accuracy.

**Evidence gap**: No source this run disclosed Abbott's CGM patient-retention/churn rate, new-patient-start market share specifically (vs total installed base share, which is a lagging indicator), or reimbursement-denial/switching-friction data directly. This is the single largest quantification gap for this specific bottleneck, parallel to the GEHC exemplar's unquantified service-attach-rate gap.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #2: Core Lab Diagnostics (Alinity) Instrument + Reagent Lock-In

### The Structure
Clinical/reference laboratories that install the Alinity platform face real switching costs: validated assay menus (regulatory/quality-system revalidation required to switch), staff retraining, and often multi-year service/reagent supply contracts. This is the classic med-tech Core Lab razor/blade pattern, directly analogous to GEHC's Imaging installed-base service annuity.

**Why this matters for the China thesis**: Core Lab itself grew +3.6% organically in Q4 2025 even as the broader Diagnostics segment (which includes COVID-test-runoff-affected rapid/POC testing) declined [facts.md E24, E59] — suggesting the underlying installed-base moat IS holding up reasonably well, and the segment-level China VBP pressure may be concentrated more in COMPETITIVE TENDER pricing for specific instrument/reagent categories than in wholesale account loss. This was NOT independently disaggregated this run (i.e., China-specific Core Lab growth vs China-specific rapid-test decline are not separately broken out in sources reviewed) — flagged as a priority item for next refresh.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #3 (INVERTED — this is a bottleneck AGAINST Abbott): China VBP Tendering, Concentrated in Diagnostics

### The Structure
Same mechanism as identified in the GEHC exemplar: China's volume-based procurement (VBP) tendering system pools hospital/lab purchasing into centralized tenders that increasingly favor domestic-manufacture criteria in government-linked procurement, working AGAINST foreign incumbents like Abbott in the specific product categories subject to VBP.

### The Evidence
- Abbott China revenue: ~$1.91B FY2025, down ~−9.75% YoY from ~$2.11B FY2024 [facts.md E63-E64] — smaller in absolute scale and (probably, subject to the reconciliation gap in facts.md O4) smaller as a % of total revenue than GEHC's China exposure (~9.85% of total), but the same negative directional VBP mechanism, explicitly named by the company as a Diagnostics-segment-concentrated headwind [E62, E65]
- COVID-19 testing revenue runoff is EXPLICITLY named as a co-driver alongside VBP [E62] — this is a genuinely different (and more benign/transient) co-factor than GEHC's China finding, which did not have an equivalent COVID-test-runoff explanation. This means Abbott's China decline may be somewhat LESS purely-structural than GEHC's, since part of the decline is a known, one-time comp effect (COVID test demand normalizing) rather than 100% competitive share loss.

### Why This Matters for the Career-Relevant Thesis
This is the direct parallel to the GEHC dossier's central China finding, but the magnitude and composition are meaningfully different: Abbott's China Diagnostics decline is smaller in magnitude (−9.75% vs GEHC's −15%) and appears to be partially a transient COVID-test-runoff effect rather than purely competitive erosion — a genuinely more nuanced, less alarming read than GEHC's finding, though this dossier could NOT cleanly separate the two effects (VBP-driven structural share loss vs COVID-test-runoff normalization) with the sources available this run. **Recommend, for the next refresh, explicitly requesting a China-specific breakdown of Core Lab vs rapid/POC-test revenue** to determine whether the underlying installed-base Core Lab business is actually holding share in China (as the +3.6% organic Q4 2025 global Core Lab print might suggest) even as COVID-test demand normalizes — this would be a meaningfully more constructive China read than GEHC's.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #4 (NEW, POST-2026-03-23): Exact Sciences' Cologuard/Cancerguard Guideline-Inclusion Moat — Now Owned by Abbott, Distribution-Leverage Thesis Untested

### The Structure
Cologuard's own pre-existing moat (colorectal cancer screening guideline inclusion, established gastroenterologist/primary-care ordering workflows) is a genuine, real bottleneck against new liquid-biopsy/stool-DNA-test entrants — but this moat was **built by Exact Sciences before the acquisition**, not by Abbott. The open question is whether Abbott's 160-country distribution network can actually extend this US-concentrated moat internationally, particularly into markets (including China, where domestic diagnostics companies like BGI and various liquid-biopsy players are actively investing) where Exact Sciences previously had limited commercial presence.

**Why this is genuinely different from GEHC's AI-monetization gap**: GEHC's gap (does AI software command an ASP premium) is a "does this feature/technology monetize at all" question. Abbott's Exact Sciences gap is more of a "does this ALREADY-monetizing, ALREADY-moated US business travel internationally via a different company's sales channel" question — a somewhat lower-uncertainty bet in principle (Cologuard's US economics are proven), but the specific execution risk (integration, channel conflict, regulatory approval in new geographies, competing against entrenched or emerging domestic liquid-biopsy players in markets like China) is entirely unproven at Abbott, since the deal closed only ~3.5 months before this as_of date.

## Bottleneck Durability vs Trend — The Core Tension

**The bull case**: FreeStyle Libre's installed base + recurring-sensor-revenue mechanism is a genuinely strong, proven, multi-year moat that continues growing (even if decelerating slightly). Core Lab's underlying organic growth (+3.6%) suggests the installed-base moat there is intact despite headline segment-level China/COVID noise. Cologuard is a proven, moated US franchise that Abbott is now positioned to potentially extend globally via genuinely superior distribution infrastructure. Structural heart/electrophysiology continues double-digit growth on real technology differentiation.

**The bear case / kill path**: Dexcom continues taking new-CGM-patient share (even if not existing-installed-base share) given its accuracy edge, gradually eroding Abbott's long-term CGM leadership position. China VBP pressure in Diagnostics could prove more structural (less COVID-runoff-driven) than currently assumed once the COVID-testing comp fully laps. The Exact Sciences distribution-leverage thesis could fail to materialize if international colorectal-screening guideline adoption, reimbursement, and physician-ordering-habit-building simply take many years longer than the deal's financing timeline can absorb — leaving Abbott with ~$27B of net debt and a dilutive acquisition that underdelivers on its stated strategic rationale.

**The key open question**: Is Abbott's China Diagnostics decline primarily COVID-test-runoff normalization (transient, already mostly played out) or genuine VBP-driven structural share loss (ongoing, comparable to GEHC's China Imaging finding)? This dossier could not disaggregate the two effects with the sources available this run — the single most important item for the next refresh, directly analogous in spirit to the GEHC exemplar's PCS-root-cause open question.

## Non-Bottleneck: Nutrition

Nutrition shows no bottleneck-style competitive advantage in any source reviewed this run — declining fastest (−9.1% organic Q4 2025) with a cited contract loss and no clearly articulated turnaround narrative, PLUS an active, quantified legal-tail-risk (NEC litigation) that has no clean parallel in the GEHC exemplar. Treat as the acknowledged weak link, analogous to GEHC's Patient Care Solutions, but with an added legal dimension that makes the downside case somewhat more concrete (a specific $70M jury verdict already realized, industry-wide ~$4B aggregate exposure estimate) even if Abbott's own specific exposure remains unquantified (facts.md O6).
