## Inversion Discipline

Per Munger's inversion framework: instead of asking "why will EW succeed," ask "what would have to be true for this investment to fail, and how likely/visible is each path?" Ranked by (a) probability this run's evidence assigns, and (b) how OBSERVABLE/testable each path is before it fully plays out.

## Kill Path 1: TAVR Growth Deceleration (Core-Franchise Risk)

**The setup**: TAVR is 74% of revenue (E51/C50). Current growth is strong and ACCELERATING (Q1 2026 TAVR +14.4% YoY, E19), but TAVR has now been Edwards' core franchise for over a decade — the historically largest source of NEW growth (expanding into lower-risk/younger patient populations per guideline changes, E38) has a natural ceiling once most eligible patients are already being treated.

**How this plays out negatively**: If the guideline-driven patient-pool expansion (younger/lower-risk patients) matures faster than expected, or if CMS NCD reconsideration (E37) does NOT expand coverage as hoped, TAVR growth could decelerate from the current mid-teens pace back toward high-single-digits (closer to FY2024's own +5.8% TAVR growth, E17) — a meaningful deceleration even without any competitive share loss.

**Probability/visibility this run**: LOW-MEDIUM near-term probability (current momentum is strong, 2 consecutive guidance raises), but this is the single most WATCHABLE, quarter-by-quarter metric — TAVR segment growth rate is reported every quarter and is the highest-frequency tripwire in this entire dossier.

**Kill criterion**: TAVR constant-currency growth falls below +5% for 2 consecutive quarters without a clear guideline/reimbursement explanation → reassess whether the "expanding patient pool" thesis has matured out.

## Kill Path 2: TMTT Ramp Fails to Sustain (Second-Growth-Engine Risk)

**The setup**: TMTT is only 9% of revenue today but is guided to grow 35-45% in FY2026 (E25/E43) and is central to the bull case that Edwards has a SECOND multi-decade evidence-moat franchise, not just one. SAPIEN M3's first-mover position in transseptal TMVR (E32) is a genuine, novel market-creation event, not yet a proven annuity.

**How this plays out negatively**: TMTT growth could disappoint if (a) Abbott's competing mitral/tricuspid platforms (MitraClip/TriClip, named as the primary TMTT competitor, E39) out-execute commercially even without matching Edwards' clinical evidence depth, (b) physician adoption of transseptal TMVR proves slower than the SAPIEN M3 approval timeline suggests (a genuinely new procedure requiring new training/referral pathways), or (c) reimbursement for the newer, more complex TMTT procedures lags behind TAVR's now-mature payer relationships.

**Probability/visibility this run**: MEDIUM — this is the least-tested part of the thesis (TMTT has only ~2 years of meaningful commercial history vs. TAVR's decade-plus track record), and this run found no head-to-head TMTT-vs-Abbott market share comparison (flagged OPEN in moat_map.md).

**Kill criterion**: TMTT growth falls materially short of the guided 35-45% FY2026 range (e.g., below 20%) for 2+ consecutive quarters → the "second growth engine" thesis is called into question, and the stock's premium valuation (which is partly pricing in TMTT's continued high growth, see valuation.md) would need to be reassessed.

## Kill Path 3: China VBP-Style Policy Expansion + Venus Medtech/Peijia Medical Domestic Entrenchment (Career-Relevant, Structural Risk)

**The setup**: This is the most CAREER-RELEVANT kill path and, on the current evidence, the clearest confirmed weakness in the moat. Edwards entered China late (2020, vs domestic approvals 2017-2019, E43); Chinese domestic players (Venus Medtech, JC Medical, CardioFlow Medtech, Peijia Medical) collectively hold ~80% of the China TAVR market (E44-E45) against Edwards' ~60% GLOBAL share (E40) — almost an exact inversion.

**How this plays out negatively (already partially realized, not merely hypothetical)**: 
1. **Direct mechanism (currently active)**: Chinese domestic players compete on ~40% lower pricing (E48) plus increasingly credible own-generated long-term clinical data (Venus Medtech's cited 10-year VenusA-Valve outcomes, E48) — if China's TAVR market genuinely scales at the forecast 55.5% CAGR (E49) from its current small base, Edwards is fighting for share of a RAPIDLY GROWING pie from a structurally weak (~20% implied) starting position, rather than defending an already-strong position (as in GEHC's case).
2. **VBP-analog mechanism (NOT yet confirmed as directly applied to TAVR specifically)**: China's broader volume-based-procurement policy apparatus (which GEHC's own dossier documents as actively pressuring imaging equipment pricing) COULD extend to structural heart devices as China's TAVR market matures and draws more government pricing attention — this run found no confirmed evidence VBP has been specifically applied to TAVR/TMTT devices (as distinct from imaging equipment or drug-eluting stents, where VBP has a longer history), but this is a genuine, testable forward risk given the policy's track record of eventually reaching maturing device categories.
3. **Second-order/template risk (same structural pattern as GEHC's China thesis)**: If Edwards cannot establish meaningful China share even as the market scales, this becomes a template case study (alongside GEHC/United Imaging) for how domestic Chinese med-device players use first-mover regulatory timing + price + "good enough" clinical data to lock out multinational incumbents in a large, growing EM market — directly relevant to Nick's broader thesis on China med-device competitiveness vs. Western imaging/device incumbents.

**Probability/visibility this run**: MEDIUM-HIGH probability this remains a structurally difficult market for Edwards for the foreseeable future (the entry-timing disadvantage cannot be undone), but LOW near-term financial materiality to Edwards' CONSOLIDATED results specifically, because (a) China revenue is not even broken out separately in Edwards' own disclosure (facts.md O1) — implying it is a small enough piece of the "International" 42% bucket not to warrant separate disclosure — and (b) unlike GEHC's ALREADY-confirmed -15% YoY China revenue decline, this is Edwards NEVER having won a large China position to begin with, so there is no comparable "erosion" data point to point to, only a persistently low starting share.

**Kill criterion**: (a) Any confirmed VBP-style national tender/pricing policy specifically targeting TAVR/TMTT devices emerges → immediately reassess China as a genuine, quantified drag (not just a qualitative risk); (b) Edwards' own future disclosure breaks out China revenue separately and shows either continued sub-scale share OR a confirmed decline → treat as GEHC-equivalent evidence; (c) any of Venus Medtech/Peijia Medical/JC Medical announce a credible ex-China (especially US/Europe) regulatory filing or approval → signals the domestic-to-global expansion Nick should watch most closely as the true long-term threat (not just China-domestic share, but reverse-competition into Edwards' home markets).

## Kill Path 4: Litigation Escalation / Unidentified Legal Exposure

**The setup**: FY2025 litigation expense spiked 8x YoY ($40.4M→$325.4M, E16/E25/E32) without this run identifying the specific driving case(s) (facts.md O2). Edwards also has a recent history of losing at least one major patent case (Meril, Federal Circuit affirmed 2024-03-25, E42).

**How this plays out negatively**: If the $208.6M Q4 2025 charge reflects an ADVERSE judgment or unfavorable settlement (Edwards paying out, rather than Edwards' own enforcement costs), and if this represents an ONGOING exposure (e.g., a running royalty or a series of related claims) rather than a single resolved event, FY2026-27 GAAP earnings could see continued elevated charges, which would also cast doubt on how much of the "adjusted EPS" framework genuinely represents non-recurring items vs. structural legal costs of operating in a fiercely patent-contested device category.

**Probability/visibility this run**: LOW-MEDIUM probability of MATERIAL escalation (Edwards' net-cash balance sheet can absorb meaningful further charges without solvency concern), but genuinely UNRESOLVED — this is a "known unknown" flagged prominently rather than papered over.

**Kill criterion**: Any single quarter shows litigation expense >$100M WITHOUT a named, specific, one-time case identified in the earnings release/call → treat as an ongoing structural cost center, not a one-off, and haircut adjusted EPS estimates accordingly.

## Kill Path 5: Valuation Compression from a High-Hug Starting Point (Price-Side Risk, Not Business Risk)

**The setup**: Price is AT/near its 52-week high ($94.37, effectively equal to the Yahoo-measured 52wk high, facts.md I1) with only ~3.1% average analyst-target upside (E54) — the thinnest margin-of-safety cushion of any name reviewed in this batch so far, purely as a function of price/multiple, independent of the underlying business quality (which this run assesses as strong).

**How this plays out negatively**: Even if TAVR/TMTT execution remains excellent, a HIGH-HUG entry point means the stock has limited room for anything short of continued "beat and raise" quarters — any single quarter of merely IN-LINE (not beat) results, given trailing P/E of 51x (GAAP-depressed, but forward P/E still 30.8x, E51), could trigger meaningful multiple compression even without any fundamental deterioration.

**Probability/visibility this run**: This is a MECHANICAL, near-certain mathematical risk of buying at a 52-week high with thin analyst upside — not a probabilistic business risk, but a structural valuation-discipline concern that governs SIZING/TIMING more than the underlying investment thesis. See valuation.md for the full scenario analysis.

**Kill criterion**: N/A (this is priced-in structural risk, not an event-driven kill criterion) — governed instead by the buy-below/starter-zone discipline in valuation.md and decision_card.
