## GAAP vs. Adjusted: The Central Financial-Quality Question This Run

The FY2025 GAAP/adjusted gap is unusually wide: diluted EPS $1.81 (GAAP) vs $2.56 (adjusted) — a **41% gap**, driven almost entirely by:
- $325.4M litigation expense (vs only $40.4M FY2024 — an 8x spike, cause not identified this run, facts.md O2)
- $146.9M impairment (largely the $99.8M JenaValve write-off tied to the FTC-blocked acquisition, E30)

**Is this "clean" or "dirty" normalization?** Unlike GEV's exemplar pattern (one-off GAINS inflating reported earnings, requiring downward normalization to see true earnings power), Edwards' one-offs this run are ALL COSTS depressing GAAP earnings — meaning normalized/adjusted earnings likely UNDERSTATE, not overstate, true recurring earnings power, similar to GEHC's own one-off pattern (tariffs, supplier disruption) but at a much larger relative magnitude versus Edwards' smaller net-income base. The key financial-quality risk is NOT that adjusted earnings are fictional, but that:
1. **The litigation charge magnitude (8x YoY) is unexplained** — if a portion of the $208.6M Q4 charge represents a RECURRING cost of doing business in a highly-litigious device category (not a one-off), then the "adjusted" $2.56 EPS may be overstating true normalized earnings power by treating an ongoing cost-of-moat-defense as purely non-recurring.
2. **The JenaValve impairment IS legitimately one-off** (a specific, discrete, non-repeating M&A write-off) — this portion of the adjustment is clean.

**Verdict**: Adjusted EPS ($2.56 FY2025, guided $2.95-$3.05 FY2026) is probably a reasonably fair estimate of underlying earnings power, but carries MORE uncertainty than a typical "add back one clearly-identified one-time item" adjustment, because the specific drivers of the $208.6M Q4 litigation charge were not identified this run (O2) — recommend the next refresh specifically pull the 10-K "Legal Proceedings" (Item 3) section to assess recurrence risk.
