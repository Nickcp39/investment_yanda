## 1. Five-Soul Initial Verdicts

### 1.1 Warren Buffett — Can I hold this 10 years? Do I understand the owner earnings? Is it cheap enough?

- **Verdict: `WATCH`**
- **One-line rationale**: I understand this business very well — Edwards makes a device that keeps people's heart valves working, sells it once per patient (plus occasional valve-in-valve repeat procedures), and has spent 68 years building the clinical trial evidence that makes doctors and payers trust it over competitors. That's a business model I can explain in one sentence and believe will still exist in 10 years. But the owner-earnings math is muddied short-term by a large, not-fully-explained litigation charge (facts.md O2), and more importantly: at a forward P/E near 31x and an EV/EBITDA near 24-27x, I am being asked to pay a very full price for even a wonderful business. My own long-standing preference is a wonderful business at a FAIR price, not a wonderful business at a demanding one.
- **Verifiable public stance referenced**: Buffett's well-documented preference (per decades of Berkshire shareholder letters) for simple, understandable businesses with durable moats, combined with his equally well-documented discipline that "price is what you pay, value is what you get" and his historical reluctance to chase quality at any price.
- **Application to EW**: The clinical-evidence moat (facts.md E36-E38, moat_map.md Moat 1) is exactly the kind of hard-to-replicate, multi-decade advantage I look for. The problem is entirely on the price side: buying at the 52-week high with only ~3% analyst-target upside (facts.md E54) is not how I want to initiate a position, however much I admire the business.

### 1.2 Charlie Munger — Where does this most likely fail? Are incentives poisoned? Is there avoidable stupidity risk?

- **Verdict: `WATCH`**
- **One-line rationale**: Inverting — the two most visible failure paths are (a) China, where Edwards structurally arrived late and now holds a minority position (~20% implied) against domestic players who got the regulatory approvals first (facts.md E43-E45) — a mistake of TIMING, not necessarily current-management's making, but one that compounds the longer it goes unaddressed; and (b) the JenaValve acquisition, where management pursued a deal that a federal court agreed would create an illegal duopoly in a specific sub-indication (facts.md E27-E31) — a genuine, recent misjudgment of antitrust risk that cost $99.8M and should be weighed as a data point on management's M&A judgment, even though it clearly did not derail the core business.
- **Verifiable public stance referenced**: Munger's long-documented inversion framework ("invert, always invert") and his equally well-known emphasis on evaluating whether management's incentives and judgment, not just the business model, deserve trust — both from his Poor Charlie's Almanack talks and decades of Berkshire/Daily Journal public commentary.
- **Application to EW**: Neither failure path looks like an "avoidable stupidity" red flag at the level of, say, reckless leverage or related-party self-dealing — this is a fundamentally well-run company with one clear historical strategic gap (China timing) and one recent tactical misstep (JenaValve). Munger's own framework would say: these are the SPECIFIC things to watch closely, not reasons to avoid the stock outright, but also not reasons to relax scrutiny just because the headline growth numbers look great.

4. **K-D [Litigation proves recurring]**: Any future quarter shows litigation expense >$100M without a named, specific, one-time case identified → treat as a structural cost of operating in this patent-contested category, haircut adjusted EPS estimates accordingly (Kill Path 4).
5. **K-E [Valuation discipline]**: Price rises above ~$100 without a corresponding fundamentals upgrade (pure multiple expansion) → no-chase, reaffirm WATCH or downgrade attractiveness further (Kill Path 5).

### 3.5 Monitoring Triggers

## 5. Module Conclusion (one paragraph)

**Five souls, five votes, unanimous WATCH, zero change after critique.** Edwards Lifesciences is, on this run's evidence, a structurally excellent business: TAVR revenue growth is ACCELERATING (not decelerating), TMTT is compounding at 35-56% as a genuine second growth engine anchored by the first-mover SAPIEN M3 transseptal mitral device, the balance sheet carries a true net-cash fortress position (~$3.4B net cash, minimal debt), and the core moat mechanism — a multi-decade, PARTNER-trial-driven clinical-evidence pipeline that shapes guidelines and payer coverage — is among the hardest-to-replicate competitive advantages reviewed in this batch. The one clearly-identified structural weakness, career-relevant to Nick's China med-device thesis, is that Edwards arrived late to China (2020, vs. domestic approvals in 2017-2019) and now holds only an implied ~20% share against domestic players (Venus Medtech, Peijia Medical, JC Medical, CardioFlow Medtech) holding ~80% — an almost exact inversion of Edwards' ~60% global TAVR share, though the China TAVR market itself remains small and is forecast to grow 55.5%/yr, meaning this is an ongoing battle for a fast-growing pie rather than a settled loss or a confirmed revenue decline (unlike GEHC's already-confirmed China erosion). The verdict is WATCH not because of any doubt about the business, but because the stock trades essentially AT its own 52-week high with only ~3% average analyst-target upside, and the base-case 5-year IRR (+7.4%) falls just short of the 8% hurdle even under a conservative, modest-multiple-compression assumption — the textbook "exceptional business, price already reflects it" pattern, distinct from GEHC's more ambiguous compound-constraint case, but converging on the same disciplined conclusion: admire the business, wait for the price.
