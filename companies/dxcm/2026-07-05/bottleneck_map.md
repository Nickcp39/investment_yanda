## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #1: Prescription CGM Reimbursement + Physician-Prescribing-Habit Lock-In (THE CORE BOTTLENECK, MIRROR OF ABT'S)

### The Structure
Exactly the same mechanism identified in the ABT exemplar, from the other side: once a person with diabetes is established on DexCom's system, switching to Abbott's Libre (or vice versa) requires a new prescription, insurance/payer reauthorization, and behavior change. This is a genuine, bidirectional switching cost — it protects DexCom's existing installed base from Abbott exactly as it protects Abbott's from DexCom.

**Evidence**: DexCom holds ~44.7% US CGM share vs Abbott's ~48.5% [facts.md E64] — genuinely close to even, meaning this bottleneck is NOT currently working decisively in either direction domestically. Globally, Abbott's larger non-US distribution footprint gives it a bigger overall share (52.83% vs DexCom's 33.89%) [facts.md E64], suggesting the switching-cost bottleneck is real WITHIN each company's existing base, but new-prescription decisions (where the bottleneck has not yet formed) still flow disproportionately to whichever company has better access/distribution in a given market — a genuine, unresolved competitive dynamic, not a settled moat.

**The differentiator DexCom is explicitly betting on**: independent MARD accuracy comparison shows DexCom G7 at 8.2% vs Libre 3 at 9.2% [facts.md E65] — DexCom's institutional strategy (per its own former-CTO-now-CEO's background, operator_underwriting.md) is to win NEW prescribing decisions on this accuracy metric, since the switching-cost bottleneck itself is roughly symmetric between the two competitors.

**Evidence gap**: No source this run disclosed DexCom's own patient-retention/churn rate or new-patient-start (vs. total installed base) market share specifically — the same gap flagged in the ABT exemplar for Abbott's side of this identical bottleneck. Given both companies likely have this data internally and neither discloses it publicly, this is a structural industry-disclosure gap, not a company-specific one.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #2: Distribution-Channel Control — DexCom's Recurring, Self-Inflicted Weak Point (INVERTED — a bottleneck AGAINST DexCom, not for it)

### The Structure
Unlike a typical "bottleneck" that protects incumbent revenue, this is a genuine, repeated OPERATIONAL vulnerability specific to DexCom: the company's route-to-patient runs through a Distributor channel (85% of FY2025 revenue, facts.md E22) spanning DME providers and pharmacies that DexCom does not fully control end-to-end.

### The Evidence (two distinct, primary-source-confirmed incidents)
- **2024 crisis**: A sales-force reorganization disrupted physician-facing geographic coverage more severely than management anticipated, combined with DME-channel share loss and faster/broader rebate-eligibility rollout — together producing a ~$300M guidance shortfall and a 40% single-day stock decline [facts.md E50-E54]. This was a genuine, internally-caused distribution/channel-management failure, not an external competitive or regulatory shock.
- **May 2026 stolen-sensor incident**: Sensors designated for scrap/destruction were stolen during the destruction process and resold through an **unauthorized third-party distributor (Pharmsource)** that DexCom does not control [facts.md E55-E59]. While this specific incident traces to DexCom's own scrap/destruction process (an internal control failure) rather than a legitimate-distributor failure, the DOWNSTREAM exposure (stolen product reaching patients) occurred specifically because it flowed through the loosely-controlled secondary/gray-market distribution layer that surrounds DexCom's official DME/pharmacy network.

### Why This Matters for the Career-Relevant Thesis
This is the single most distinctive risk pattern in DexCom's profile relative to every other name in this S&P 500 medical batch reviewed so far: **two, not one, primary-source-confirmed multi-year incidents originating in the same value-chain link (distribution/channel control)**. This reads as a genuine structural management-attention gap, not coincidence — DexCom's R&D/technical execution (accuracy edge, product-cycle cadence) appears genuinely strong, but its channel/distribution oversight has now failed twice in ways that materially affected either financial results (2024) or patient safety/brand trust (2026). Recommend, for the next refresh, explicitly monitoring whether the May 2026 incident triggers any FDA quality-systems inquiry, and whether Q2 2026 results (2026-07-29) show any measurable prescriber-confidence or DME-relationship impact.

## Binding Bottleneck Candidate #3 (ABSENT/UNQUANTIFIED — this is a bottleneck neither confirmed FOR nor AGAINST DexCom): China Competitive Emergence

### The Structure
DexCom's own 10-K explicitly names "emerging competitors primarily located in China" as a risk factor [facts.md E61] — the same category of concern the ABT and GEHC exemplars found INDEPENDENTLY QUANTIFIED (China VBP tendering, specific China revenue figures). For DexCom, this bottleneck candidate is genuinely **unresolved, not merely under-quantified**: DexCom's US/International-only geographic disclosure (facts.md E23) means there is no company-sourced data at all on China-specific revenue, market share, or competitive dynamics.

### Why This Matters
This is the most significant career-relevant OPEN item across this entire dossier. If China's domestic CGM manufacturers ARE meaningfully emerging (as DexCom's own risk factor implies), DexCom currently has the least internal visibility into (and the least disclosed exposure to) that threat of any company in this batch — worse than ABT's reconciliation-caveat-but-at-least-quantified China figure, and worse than GEHC's cleaner China VBP disclosure. This should be the top priority research gap to close at the next refresh, ideally via dedicated search on specific Chinese CGM entrants (e.g., Sinocare, Micro Tech Medical, POCTech) and their relative technical/regulatory maturity versus DexCom/Abbott's global-standard products — directly relevant to Nick's China med-device career lens.
