# Bottleneck Map — CEG

Last updated: 2026-06-19

Purpose: identify where scarcity creates pricing power, strategic control, or vulnerability — and answer the LEAD question: **is CEG's existing nuclear fleet a DURABLE scarcity rent, or is the AI-power premium already fully priced and hostage to a single regulatory switch (FERC co-location)?**

---

## TL;DR — the LEAD question, answered

**The rent is durable and real; it is NOT primarily hostage to FERC; the −34% de-rate is mostly multiple normalization (>35x → ~24x fwd) + Calpine digestion — not a thesis break.** The premise's "or" is a false binary: the scarcity is structural *and* the regulatory switch already flipped favorable.

1. **The scarce asset is physical and un-buildable.** ~22 GW of existing carbon-free nuclear baseload, ~95% CF, ~$0 marginal fuel. **No new large US reactor has come on time/budget in a generation; greenfield permit+build ≈ 10–15 years.** The supply curve cannot respond to AI demand → the scarcity rent is **structural**. (C022, C004)

2. **The scariest pre-registered kill — FERC blocking co-location — is empirically DEFUSED.** A **5-0 FERC order (2025-12-18, 193 FERC ¶61,217, Docket EL25-49) directs PJM to ENABLE co-located load** — the opposite of the Nov-2024 Talen/AWS BTM rejection that originally framed this as *the* swing risk. (C015)

3. **CEG's flagship sidesteps the fight anyway.** Crane→Microsoft is **front-of-the-meter** (Crane injects to the grid; MSFT matches its PJM load) — not behind-the-meter. So even the residual definitional risk in PJM compliance largely does not touch CEG's marquee deal. (C013)

4. **The legislated downside floor survived OBBBA.** §45U PTC intact: ~$25/MWh gross-receipts floor to 2032. 2027 open nuclear is priced **~$34/MWh** — above the floor. (C019, C026)

5. **What's actually left is execution + a capped flagship.** Calpine levered the balance sheet to ~3.8x (deleverage to ~2.0x by 2027 is a *forecast*) and diluted carbon-free purity 90%→58%; and Crane's upside is **already sold to MSFT**, so the *equity* re-rate hinges on the *next* AI PPA. (C011, C023, C013)

> **One-line answer:** the rent is durable and the two scariest kills (FERC, PTC) are resolved favorably; the price paid for the rent merely normalized. The live risks are *execution* (Calpine deleverage) and a flagship whose upside is already spoken for — not the existence of the moat.

## The "is it priced / FERC-hostage?" question, dissected

**Three distinct worries get conflated under the LEAD question:**

| If the worry is… | Verdict for CEG | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Is the AI-power premium fully priced? | **Partly — but the froth, not the moat, was cut** | >35x → ~24x fwd is multiple normalization; ~24x vs 16–18x peers = fair, not cheap (C027, C028) |
| Is it hostage to the FERC co-location switch? | **No — the switch flipped favorable** | 5-0 enabling order; front-of-meter flagship sidesteps it (C015, C013) |
| Is the downside open-ended (power prices)? | **No — legislatively floored** | §45U ~$25/MWh to 2032; 2027 open ~$34/MWh (C019, C026) |

**Does CEG own the bottleneck or merely depend on it?** It **owns** it — the scarce input (existing carbon-free baseload) sits on CEG's balance sheet. This is the key contrast with NBIS (which *depends on* a build-and-energize chain it doesn't own). CEG's dependence is on *market rules* (resolved favorable) and *its own deleverage* (a forecast), not on a scarce input controlled by others.

## Questions (the template's bottleneck tests)

- **Can competitors buy around the bottleneck?** **No — that's the point.** You cannot build a new large US reactor on any relevant horizon. VST/TLN/NRG own *some* nuclear too, so the rent is shared among existing owners — but it cannot be *added to*. Being a large, low-cost existing owner is the durable position. (C022)
- **Temporary or structural?** **Structural.** The un-buildability is effectively permanent on the physics and legislated to 2032+ on the PTC. This is the opposite of NBIS's *temporary* throughput bottleneck (which eases as supply chains catch up).
- **Does the company own the bottleneck or merely depend on it?** **Owns it.** (See above.)
- **Does solving/harvesting it improve economics or destroy returns via capex?** **Improves them — no capex treadmill.** Harvesting the rent = running an already-built fleet at ~95% CF and signing PPAs; Crane is a ~$1.6B DOE-funded restart, not a greenfield. (C018) This is structurally better than the NBIS case, where solving the bottleneck costs $20–25B/yr.

## Risk — where the bottleneck is still a vulnerability

The moat is durable, but the *equity* carries three specific, named risks (none of which is "the moat broke"):

1. **The flagship's upside is capped.** Crane→MSFT is contracted at an undisclosed (likely fixed) price → CEG's own upside on its marquee asset is limited. The re-rate depends on the **next** PPA, which is unsigned. (C013)
2. **Calpine execution.** ~3.8x leverage + purity dilution; deleverage to ~2.0x is a forecast. A stall here re-rates the whole entity toward a merchant-gas multiple. (C011, C023)
3. **Power-price compression + a residual FERC definition.** Curves toward the ~$25 floor thin the premium (downside floored, upside gone); and the final PJM co-location *definition* is still litigated (a minor industry-BTM risk; CEG's front-of-meter flagship is insulated). (C026, C016)

**Bottom line:** the bottleneck is a **durable, self-owned scarcity rent** — the LEAD answer is "durable, not FERC-hostage." The −34% cut the froth and the Calpine overhang, not the moat. The honest reason this is a STARTER and not a layup: the *equity* re-rate now leans on an unseen next PPA + a clean Calpine deleverage, and at ~24x you are paying fair, not cheap.
