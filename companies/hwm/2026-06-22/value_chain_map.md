## Value-capture logic

- **Upstream (materials):** HWM buys Ni/Co/Ti/Al alloys. Some input-cost and tariff exposure (flagged ~cost headwind), but pass-through/contract structure + spec position protect margin.
- **HWM node (the choke point):** Casting single-crystal superalloy turbine airfoils to aerospace tolerances is one of the hardest manufacturing problems in the world — yield, metallurgy, and certification are the barriers. HWM + a very small set of peers (incl. captive OEM capacity) hold the qualified positions. This is why Engine Products earns 36.6% EBITDA.
- **Downstream (OEMs):** Concentrated, powerful customers (GE/Safran/RTX/Rolls/Boeing/Airbus). They have buying power on NEW parts, but switching a qualified airfoil supplier mid-program is slow, costly, and risky — so the relationship is sticky and re-prices with inflation.
- **Aftermarket (the prize):** Once an engine is in service for 20-30 years, it consumes replacement airfoils at high margin. HWM's growing installed base = a compounding spares annuity that is far less OEM-price-pressured than original equipment.

## Pricing power evidence

- 30%+ segment EBITDA in 3 of 4 segments; company margin 32.0% (+320bps YoY).
- Engine Products margin +400bps YoY — mix (spares) + volume + pricing.
- Qualification gating + cost-of-failure asymmetry (a $200 fastener can ground a $400M aircraft) = structural pricing power.

## Where the chain is weakest

- **Engineered Structures (22.4%):** more commoditized Ti forming, lumpier, OEM-concentrated.
- **Forged Wheels (30.5% but cyclical):** strong share but tied to the (currently declining) heavy-truck build cycle.
