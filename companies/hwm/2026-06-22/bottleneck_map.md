# HWM Bottleneck Map (M3)

As-of: 2026-06-22

## The bottleneck question: who holds the scarce resource?

In the jet-engine value chain, the scarcest resource is **qualified hot-section capacity** — the ability to cast single-crystal superalloy turbine airfoils that survive >1,400degC combustion gas at full certification. HWM is one of a handful of holders. This is the node where economics concentrate.

## Bottleneck stack

| Layer | Scarce? | Who holds it | HWM position |
|---|---|---|---|
| Superalloy/Ti raw metal | Somewhat (supply chain) | Specialty metals producers | Buyer; some cost/tariff exposure |
| **Single-crystal airfoil casting** | **YES — the choke point** | HWM + few peers + captive OEM | **Leading independent** |
| Engine assembly | Less so | GE/Safran/RTX/Rolls | Customer |
| Aftermarket spares | Scarce (qualified parts) | OEMs + HWM | **Growing installed-base annuity** |
| Forged wheels | Moderate | HWM (#1) + few | Leader, cyclical |

## Why this matters for the thesis

- The bottleneck explains the 36.6% Engine Products margin and the durability of the spares annuity — economics sit at the scarce node, not at assembly or distribution.
- It also explains why HWM re-prices with inflation and is not commoditized: the customer cannot easily route around the qualified supplier.

## Bottleneck risk

- The bottleneck is **volume-cyclical, not structurally fragile**: an aero down-cycle reduces throughput but does not remove HWM from the qualified position. So the bottleneck protects the business through a cycle but does NOT protect the *multiple* — which is the actual risk here (M5/M6).

## One metric that forces a market update

**Engine spares revenue growth + commercial-aero OEM build-rate trajectory.** If both keep compounding, the durability premium is validated; if spares roll over (e.g. an air-traffic/geopolitical shock) or build rates are cut, the cyclical-peak-multiple trap (M5) starts to bite.
