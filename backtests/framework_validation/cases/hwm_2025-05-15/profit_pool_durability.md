## 1. Value Chain — where the economics sit

```
Specialty alloys ─► [HWM: cast/forge qualified airfoils, fasteners, structures] ─► Engine OEMs (CFM/GE,
   (nickel,Ti)        ▲ flight-safety-critical, decades-qualified                    P&W/RTX, Rolls) ─► Airframers
                       │                                                              (Boeing, Airbus) ─► Airlines
                       └──────────────── AFTERMARKET SPARES (per engine flight-hour, multi-decade) ◄──┘
```

The two fat-margin nodes are (1) **engine content** (Engine Products: HWM's largest and fastest segment,
$996M Q1'25 +13%, $3.735B FY24 +14%; S003/S001) and (2) the **spares annuity** layered on the installed base
(~20% of revenue, +40% in aero/defense; S004). HWM sits on **both**. The OEMs and airlines capture huge
*revenue* but face their own cost/cyclicality; HWM captures a **certified, high-margin slice** with far less
balance-sheet risk than an airframer or airline.

## 2. Bottleneck / Moat — why it persists

- **Qualification / certification switching costs.** Single-crystal turbine airfoils and aero-grade fasteners are
  flight-safety-critical and qualified on *specific* engine platforms (LEAP, GTF, GE9X, F135). Requalifying a new
  supplier is a multi-year, regulator-gated process an OEM will not undertake for an incumbent that delivers — the
  classic aerospace moat. Result: **sole/dual-source content positions** on the highest-volume programs.
- **Process know-how / yield.** Casting single-crystal superalloy blades at scale is a low-yield, hard-won craft.
  HWM's Q1'25 **Structures margin +720 bps to 21.3% on "improved process control"** and **>100% incremental EBITDA
  flow-through** (S004) are direct evidence of proprietary process advantage, not just cycle.
- **Installed-base annuity.** Every engine HWM is qualified on generates **decades** of spares demand tied to flight
  hours — a recurring, higher-margin stream that is **de-coupled from OEM build lumpiness** and grows with the fleet.
- **Pricing power.** **COGS fell to 68.9% of sales in FY24 from 71.9% in FY23** (S002) on "favorable product pricing"
  — HWM is taking price through the cycle, the signature of a constrained, qualified supplier.

Durability evidence in one number: **adj EBITDA margin 25.8% FY24 → 28.8% Q1'25** while *de-levering* — margins
expanding and balance-sheet risk falling simultaneously.

## 4. Why HWM captures the profit pool (synthesis)

HWM is the **toll-booth on engine content + spares** for the two engine/airframe duopolies. It captures the pool
because (a) it is *qualified* where requalification is prohibitively slow, (b) it *innovates the process* (yield/
margin gains), (c) it *owns the aftermarket annuity* on a growing installed base, and (d) it is run by an operator
who converts that into expanding margins, falling leverage, and shrinking share count. This is a **+2 durability**
profile.

## 5. The one honest reservation (carried to M4/M5/M6)

Durability of the *moat* is high; durability of *current earnings power* is **cyclical**. The 28.8% margin and
>100% incrementals reflect a strong up-cycle as well as structural gains — through a downturn, volumes and
incrementals would compress (though spares + the IG balance sheet cushion the floor). This is **not** a mark
against M3 (the moat persists through cycles); it is the reason M4/M5/M6 size from *normalized* economics and a
disciplined price, rather than extrapolating peak incrementals.
