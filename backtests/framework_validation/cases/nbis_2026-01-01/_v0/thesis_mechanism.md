## 2. First-Principles Check

- **Is the bottleneck real and durable?** Yes for the medium term. Power interconnects and
  data-center builds take years (S020); GPUs are allocated, not freely available. A player
  that has already contracted ~2.5GW and secured NVIDIA supply holds a scarce position that
  capital alone cannot instantly replicate.
- **Is the demand real or a bubble artifact?** The buyers are the most creditworthy companies
  on earth (Microsoft, Meta) plus a sold-out core book. Even if AI capex moderates, multi-year
  committed contracts de-risk near-term utilization. The risk is over-building into a later
  glut, not near-term demand absence.
- **Does NBIS specifically capture the rent, or just the cycle?** The vertical integration and
  the hyperscaler wins argue NBIS captures more than a commodity reseller. But it is not a
  monopoly; CoreWeave, Crusoe, Lambda, and the hyperscalers' own clouds compete. This is a
  fast-growing share-taker in a scarce-supply market, not an unassailable moat -> M3 must size that.
- **Why is it "cheap" relative to its own story?** Not optically cheap on trailing numbers
  (loss-making, ~$21B cap on ~$146M quarterly revenue). It is "cheap" only if you underwrite
  the forward ARR ramp and believe the build gets funded. The mispricing is a *narrative/
  time-horizon* gap (ex-Yandex stigma + dilution fear vs. a contracted demand book), not a
  classic low-multiple value gap.

## 5. Kill Conditions (handoff to M5)

1. Core ARR growth decelerates sharply or guidance to the $7-9B 2026 exit is walked back.
2. Contracted power fails to energize/convert to revenue on the guided schedule.
3. The ~2.5GW build can only be financed on heavily dilutive or distressed terms.
4. A marquee customer (Microsoft/Meta) renegotiates, delays materially, or cancels.
5. Core adjusted-EBITDA margin reverses back toward break-even or below.
