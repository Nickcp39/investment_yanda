# M5 Inversion / Trap Test — MU as of 2025-03-21

Role: risk / veto. Signal: **-1**. Confidence: **med**. **hard_veto: NO.**

How is this a permanent-loss or value-trap mistake?

## Trap path 1 — Cyclical-peak trap (the primary risk)
Buying a memory cyclical when run-rate earnings are well above mid-cycle and calling ~15× "cheap." If the up-cycle rolls over (hyperscaler digestion, commodity DRAM/NAND oversupply), earnings can fall toward or below normalized, the multiple re-rates against falling EPS, and the stock can halve even though the company is "fine." **Evidence this risk is live:** FQ2 revenue already ticked down QoQ and GM compressed; consumer markets called out as weak; the stock fell ~8% on the print. [S1, S5] **Severity: high. This is the dominant trap and it sets the size.**

## Trap path 2 — Oversupply / capex black hole
Memory is a fixed-cost commodity business; all three players are racing HBM and leading-edge capacity. If the industry over-builds (as it has every prior cycle), ASPs collapse, utilization falls, and Micron burns through an up-cycle's FCF. Micron's own FCF is only $857M in a strong quarter against $3.1B/qtr capex — there is little cushion if pricing turns. CHIPS-Act fabs lock in capex regardless of cycle. [S1, S3] **Severity: medium-high, structural to the industry.**

## Trap path 3 — HBM competition eroding the premium
The HBM bottleneck rent is contestable. Samsung and SK Hynix are qualifying/ramping HBM into the same accelerator platforms. If supply catches demand or a competitor wins the leading socket, the premium that is driving CNBU's margin recovery compresses, and the "structural mix shift raises mid-cycle" thesis weakens. [S3, first-principles] **Severity: medium. This is the key threat to the bull's normalized-earnings step-up.**

## Trap path 4 — False-cheap on peak/trough earnings
Two-sided. Trailing GAAP P/E (~136×) makes it look absurdly expensive; run-rate P/E (~15×) makes it look cheap. **Both are traps.** The honest read (M4) is normalized P/E ~21–35×. An investor who anchors on the 15× run-rate is making the classic cyclical-peak mistake; one who anchors on 136× trailing wrongly rejects it. The decision must be made on normalized economics and downside survivability, not either headline multiple.

## Trap path 5 — Balance-sheet survival
**Largely clears.** Net debt ~$4.76B, net-debt/equity ~0.10, debt termed out, $9.6B liquidity, government capex support. Micron can survive a normal downcycle without dilution or distress. This is NOT INTC/turnaround-style survival risk. [S1] → This is why **no hard veto fires**: the permanent-impairment / solvency condition is absent.

## Does a hard_veto fire? → **NO**
Per WEIGHTING_HARNESS §6, hard veto fires on profit-pool loss, normalized earnings far below trailing with capex absorbing owner earnings AND structural decline / unsurvivable downside. Here:
- Profit pool is **growing** (data-center/HBM), not eroding → no §6 trigger.
- Normalized earnings are *below run-rate* but the company is solidly profitable mid-cycle and the balance sheet survives → capex strain is a haircut, not a death spiral.
- This is `cyclical_inflection`, not `structural_decline_trap` or `false_cheap_value_trap`. The harness gate for cyclical_inflection vetoes only "if balance sheet cannot survive cycle" — it can. **No veto.**

## What would prove us wrong (falsifiers)
1. Two consecutive quarters of declining data-center/HBM revenue and gross margin (up-cycle rolling over).
2. HBM ASP/premium compression as competitor capacity floods in.
3. Industry capex announcements signalling oversupply into 2026.
4. Inventory building faster than revenue → NRV write-down risk returning.

## M5 net
**-1 (warning, not veto).** The cyclical-peak / oversupply / HBM-competition risks are real and cap the position size hard, but balance-sheet survivability and a growing (not eroding) profit pool keep this out of veto territory. The penalty should shrink the size and tighten the buy-below, not zero it.
