# M3 Profit Pool / Durability — HWM 2024-05-15

Role: **conviction**. Who captures the economics, why it persists, who runs it. Only info ≤ 2024-05-15.

## 0. Module Verdict

- **M3 signal: +2** (a genuinely wide-moat, hard-to-replicate position on the highest-value engine node, with a
  rising recurring-aftermarket mix, run by a disciplined capital allocator — the durability case is strong and
  multi-pillared). The only thing that gives pause is *cyclicality of demand*, which is an M4/M5 concern, not a
  moat-erosion concern.
- Confidence: **high**.

## 1. Value chain — where does the margin sit?

```
Specialty alloys (Ni superalloy, Ti) ─► HOWMET (cast/forge/machine certified hot-section parts, fasteners,
   (commodity-ish inputs)                 structures, wheels) ─► Engine OEMs (GE, Pratt/RTX, Safran/CFM) +
                                          the qualified, certified node                Airframers (Boeing, Airbus)
                                                                                       ─► Airlines (new-build + SPARES)
```

- The **rent-earning node is Howmet's**: turning commodity-ish superalloy/titanium into **flight-certified,
  single-crystal turbine airfoils/blades and structural castings**. The metallurgy (directional solidification,
  single-crystal growth, coatings) and the **per-program safety certification** are the value — not the metal.
- The richest tier is the **aftermarket spare**: the same certified part, sold repeatedly over a 20–30-year engine
  life at high margin, with the OEM/airline locked to the qualified source. FY2023 Engine Products already earns a
  **27.2% segment EBITDA margin** (S001) — evidence the spread is fat, not thin.

## 2. The bottleneck (the M3 core question)

- **Binding constraint = qualified, certified hot-section manufacturing capacity.** A new entrant cannot simply
  "buy GPUs" here — it must develop the metallurgy, build the casting capacity, and pass **multi-year per-engine-
  program qualification** under flight-safety scrutiny. That is the moat.
- Reinforced by a **system-wide input constraint**: titanium (Russian-VSMPO dependence, deliberately sanction-
  exempted; Airbus diversified to ~20% — S018), casting capacity, and skilled labor all cap how fast *anyone* can
  ramp. Scarcity of qualified supply **supports pricing and share** for the already-qualified incumbent.
- This is the **good kind** of bottleneck to *own* (you are the scarce node) — the opposite of NBIS, which had to
  *clear* a capacity bottleneck. Howmet **is** the bottleneck others depend on.

## 3. Moat / durability — how durable is the spread?

Durability factors **FOR** (why this is +2):
- **Certified, sole/dual-sourced incumbency** across the major engine programs — switching means re-qualifying a
  flight-critical part, which OEMs avoid. Decades-long relationships; GE ~12% and RTX ~9% of sales are *partnerships*
  (S001), not concentration risk in the fragile sense.
- **Rising recurring-aftermarket mix** — spares are higher-margin and recurring over the engine's multi-decade life;
  this structurally lifts through-cycle margins (the 24% record Q1'24 EBITDA margin and 35% incrementals — S003,
  S005 — are partly mix, not just volume).
- **Installed-base annuity** — every engine Howmet has content on is a multi-decade replacement-parts stream
  largely independent of the *new-build* cycle. The record OEM backlog (~11–12yr, S015) keeps *expanding* that base.
- **Operating leverage off a fixed qualified base** — 35% incremental EBITDA margins (S005) mean revenue growth
  drops disproportionately to FCF.
- **Diversification** across four segments (engine/fasteners/structures/wheels) and across OE + aftermarket +
  defense + IGT smooths any single end-market shock.

Durability factors **AGAINST** (the honest caveats — but note these are *cyclical/price*, not *moat-erosion*):
- **Demand cyclicality** — aerospace is cyclical; a future traffic/backlog reversal would hit volumes (an M4/M5
  concern, addressed there). The *moat* doesn't erode, but the *earnings* can swing.
- **Margin-peak risk** — record 24% EBITDA margin could prove cyclically high; the durability of the *current* spread
  level (vs the existence of the spread) is the open question (M1 Q4).
- **Customer power** — GE/RTX/Safran/Boeing/Airbus are sophisticated, concentrated buyers who push on price; the
  moat holds because re-qualification is costly, but pricing is negotiated, not dictated.
- **Titanium/geopolitical tail** — a titanium supply shock would be double-edged (caps volume, supports price).

## 4. Operator → durability ceiling (fold-in)

**Operator grade ≈ 4.5/5 (strong).** Per S007 and the filings' capital-allocation record:
- **John Plant** (CEO & Executive Chairman) has run Howmet since the 2020 Arconic separation and has a **demonstrated
  capital-allocation track record**: **debt cut $456M in FY2023 → investment grade at all three agencies** (Feb-2024
  Moody's upgrade), **buybacks at lower prices** ($66.87 avg in Q1'24 — i.e., he bought *below* the as-of $82.75),
  and a **40% dividend increase** funded by genuine FCF (not debt). This is textbook owner-operator behavior — return
  cash, delever, repurchase counter-cyclically.
- **Lifts conviction:** disciplined, value-aware capital allocation materially de-risks the *per-share* compounding
  case; a great franchise can still disappoint shareholders under a poor allocator — Howmet's allocator is a clear
  asset, and it is the main reason M3 is +2 rather than +1.
- **Caps the ceiling (counted once, within-band):** Plant is **72** — key-man/succession is a real, if not imminent,
  durability discount; and a high-quality business at a high multiple still depends on management *not* overpaying
  for M&A or chasing growth at the expense of returns. No evidence of either at as-of; flagged as a monitor.

## 5. Profit-pool verdict

- **Is there a real, capturable profit pool?** Yes — certified hot-section content + a recurring high-margin
  aftermarket annuity is one of the best profit pools in industrials, and Howmet sits squarely on the rent node.
- **Is the capture durable?** **Yes, structurally** — certification moat + installed-base annuity + rising spares mix
  + a disciplined allocator. The caveat is *cyclical demand* and *margin-peak* risk (handled in M4/M5), **not** moat
  erosion. There is no sign the competitive position is weakening; if anything the aftermarket mix is strengthening it.
- **Net: +2.** A wide-moat franchise capturing a high-quality, partly-recurring profit pool, run by a proven capital
  allocator. The conviction on *business quality and durability* is genuinely high; the discipline must come on
  *price and cycle* (M4/M5/M6), not on the moat.
